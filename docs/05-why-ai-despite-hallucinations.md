# Why AI Despite Hallucinations

## Yes, AI Hallucinates

Let's not pretend otherwise. Large language models make things up. They state false things with absolute confidence. They invent meeting dates, fabricate quotes, and occasionally hallucinate entire workstreams that don't exist.

I once asked an AI to summarize a meeting note and it confidently attributed a decision to someone who wasn't even at the meeting. The name appeared elsewhere in the repo. The AI connected dots that didn't exist. Classic hallucination.

If you're waiting for AI to be perfectly reliable before using it, you'll be waiting a long time. But you'll also be spending 90 minutes every morning drowning in context while you wait.

## When Hallucinations Matter

Hallucinations are dangerous when:

- **The AI is your only source of truth** — you ask ChatGPT about your program and trust the answer without checking
- **There's no grounding data** — the AI is generating from its training data, not your documents
- **The output goes directly to stakeholders** — no human review step
- **Specifics matter** — exact dates, exact numbers, exact quotes

In these cases, a hallucination can cause real damage. Wrong date in a stakeholder email. Fabricated decision attributed to someone who wasn't in the meeting. Made-up metric in a dashboard.

## When Hallucinations Don't Matter (Much)

Hallucinations are a manageable risk when:

- **The AI is summarizing YOUR documents** — it might miss something, but it's not inventing from scratch
- **You're using the output as a draft** — you review and edit before it goes anywhere
- **The task is synthesis, not recall** — "what are the themes across these 10 meeting notes?" is more forgiving than "what exact date did we agree to?"
- **The alternative is worse** — getting 85% of the picture in 2 minutes beats getting 60% of the picture in 90 minutes

## The Key Insight

Here's what makes Palimpsest different from "just using ChatGPT":

**The AI is reading YOUR docs. Not the internet. Not its training data. Your actual, current, program-specific files.**

This fundamentally changes the hallucination risk profile. When you ask "what are the current blockers?" the AI is reading your DASHBOARD.md and your meeting notes. It might miss a nuance or slightly mischaracterize a blocker, but it's not going to invent a blocker that doesn't exist in any of your files.

The grounding data is your repo. The more complete and current your repo is, the lower the hallucination risk.

## Guardrails That Work

### Cite Sources

Train yourself (and configure your AI) to ask for citations. "What are the blockers? Cite which file each one comes from."

Now you can verify. If the AI says "according to meeting-notes/2026-03-05-arch-review.md, the API migration is blocked on schema changes" — you can check that file. Takes 10 seconds.

### Human Review Is Non-Negotiable

Nothing generated by AI goes to stakeholders without human review. Period. This isn't a nice-to-have. It's the whole model.

The AI drafts. You review, edit, and send. The AI saves you 80% of the effort. You provide the 20% that requires judgment, context, and accountability.

### Structured Prompts Reduce Risk

Vague prompts get hallucinated answers. Specific prompts get grounded answers.

Bad: "Tell me about the program status."
Good: "Read DASHBOARD.md and summarize the status of each workstream. Only include information from that file."

The more specific your prompt, the less room the AI has to fill gaps with invention.

### Cross-Reference on High-Stakes Items

For anything going to leadership, a client, or into a decision: ask the AI, then verify the key claims manually. This sounds like it defeats the purpose, but it doesn't — verifying 5 specific claims takes 5 minutes. Building the whole update from scratch takes an hour.

## The Alternative Is Worse

Let's be honest about the alternative. Without AI assistance, you're:

- Spending 90 minutes every morning on context gathering
- Missing things because humans are bad at synthesizing across 40 documents
- Writing updates manually, which means you do it less often
- Relying on memory, which is its own form of hallucination (we just don't call it that)

Human memory is unreliable. Human attention is limited. Human synthesis across large document sets is slow and incomplete.

The question isn't "is AI perfect?" The question is "is AI + human review better than human alone?" The answer, in practice, is yes. Significantly.

## A Framework for Trust Calibration

Not all AI outputs deserve the same level of scrutiny. Here's a practical framework:

| Output Type | Trust Level | Review Needed |
|------------|------------|---------------|
| Brainstorming, draft outlines | High trust | Skim it |
| Internal summaries for your own use | Medium-high trust | Quick sanity check |
| Status updates for team | Medium trust | Read carefully, verify key claims |
| Stakeholder communications | Low trust | Verify every specific claim |
| Anything with dates, numbers, or quotes | Very low trust | Manually confirm each one |

The goal isn't to trust or distrust AI categorically. It's to calibrate your review effort to the stakes of the output.

## Privacy Considerations

One legitimate concern: what data is the AI seeing?

With Palimpsest:

- **Local-first**: your repo is on your machine. If you use a local model (Ollama, etc.), data never leaves your laptop.
- **Scoped context**: the AI sees your program repo, not your entire company. You control what goes in.
- **Private files stay private**: the `.gitignore` ensures sensitive files aren't committed. The AI reads them locally but they don't go to GitHub.
- **Model choice matters**: if your program involves sensitive data, use a model provider whose data handling policies your company has approved.

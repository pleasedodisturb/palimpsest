# Reducing Status Meetings

How to stop using meetings as information exchange and reclaim everyone's calendars.

---

## The Problem

Most status meetings follow the same pattern: each person gives a verbal update, others half-listen, someone asks a clarifying question, and an hour later everyone goes back to work with information they could have read in 5 minutes.

Status meetings are the most expensive way to share information. A 1-hour meeting with 8 people costs 8 hours of productivity, plus context-switching overhead. If the information could have been shared in writing, that's 8 hours wasted every week. Multiply by the number of recurring meetings on your calendar.

## The Alternative

Replace information exchange with async reporting. Keep meetings for things that actually require real-time interaction.

### What Becomes Async

- Status updates (what happened, what's next)
- Metrics and dashboards
- Progress reports
- Risk and blocker visibility
- Routine announcements

### What Stays as Meetings

- Active problem-solving requiring back-and-forth
- Relationship building and team cohesion
- Decisions requiring real-time debate
- Retrospectives and feedback sessions
- Onboarding conversations

## How to Transition

### Step 1: Audit Your Meetings

List every recurring meeting. For each one, ask:
- What information is exchanged?
- What decisions are made?
- What would happen if we cancelled it?

If the answer to the third question is "people would be less informed," that's an information distribution problem, not a meeting problem.

### Step 2: Establish Async Channels

| Channel | Purpose | Update Frequency |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Status channel | Broadcast updates, metrics, progress | Daily or weekly |
| Team channel | Working discussions, quick questions | Continuous |
| Decision log | Document decisions and rationale | As decisions are made |
| Dashboard | Visual metrics, project health | Auto-updated or weekly |

### Step 3: Set the Cadence

| Day | Async Activity | Who |
|-----|---------------|-----|
| Monday | Post weekly priorities | Team leads |
| Wednesday | Mid-week pulse (blockers only) | Anyone blocked |
| Friday | Post weekly update | TPM / Program lead |

### Step 4: Kill the Meeting (Gradually)

Don't cancel everything at once. Start with one meeting:
1. Replace it with an async update for 2 weeks
2. Check: did anyone miss the meeting? Did information flow?
3. If yes, make it permanent. Move to the next meeting
4. If no, investigate what was lost and address the gap

## Communication Norms

### For Writers

- Lead with the headline — don't bury the point
- Use structure — bullets, tables, headers
- Be specific — "we're behind" vs "we're 3 days behind on the API integration due to a dependency delay"
- Tag people only when you need their action
- Set deadlines for responses when input is needed

### For Readers

- Check the status channel at least once daily
- Respond within 24 hours when tagged
- Use reactions/emoji to acknowledge without cluttering the thread
- Ask clarifying questions in-thread, not in DMs

### For Leaders

- Model the behavior — post your own updates async
- Don't schedule a meeting to discuss something that was posted async
- Resist the urge to "just hop on a quick call" for information exchange
- Celebrate when the team resolves something async that used to require a meeting

## Measuring Success

- Number of recurring meetings (should decrease)
- Meeting hours per person per week (should decrease)
- Time from question to answer (should stay the same or improve)
- "I didn't know about that" moments (should decrease)
